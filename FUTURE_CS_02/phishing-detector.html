Real Phishing Examples

Study these real-world phishing email examples to understand how attackers operate and what red flags to look for.

Example 1 — PayPal Account Suspension Scam

PHISHING
From: security-alert@paypa1-verify.net
To: user@email.com
Subject: ⚠️ URGENT: Your PayPal Account Has Been SUSPENDED
Dear Valued Customer, We detected suspicious activity on your account. Your PayPal has been temporarily LIMITED. Verify your identity within 24 hours or your account will be PERMANENTLY CLOSED and funds frozen. 👉 Restore Access Now: http://paypa1-secure-verify.ru/restore?id=8823 WARNING: Failure to act will result in permanent termination. PayPal Security Team

🚩 Red Flags Identified

✕Spoofed domain: "paypa1-verify.net" mimics PayPal using number "1" instead of letter "l"
✕Malicious URL: Russian domain (.ru) with suspicious path — not PayPal's real site
✕Urgency + fear: "24 hours", "PERMANENTLY CLOSED", all-caps language to panic user
✕Generic greeting: "Dear Valued Customer" — PayPal always uses your real name
⚡Threat of loss: Threatening to freeze funds to force immediate action

Example 2 — HR Benefits Portal Phish

SUSPICIOUS
From: hr-benefits@company-portal.info
To: employee@yourcompany.com
Subject: Action Required: 2026 Benefits Enrollment Closing Friday
Hello, Your annual benefits enrollment window closes this Friday at 5 PM. Please log in to confirm your selections. Failure to enroll will result in default coverage. Access the portal: https://company-portal.info/enroll/2026 Contact HR at hr@yourcompany.com if you have questions. Human Resources Department

🚩 Red Flags Identified

✕Suspicious domain: "company-portal.info" is not a corporate domain — real HR uses your company's own domain
⚡Deadline pressure: "Closing Friday" creates mild urgency to discourage verification
⚡Mixed domains: Link goes to .info site but contact email uses @yourcompany.com — inconsistency
⚡Generic greeting: "Hello" with no employee name — internal HR emails include your name

Example 3 — Fake Microsoft Login Alert

PHISHING
From: no-reply@micros0ft-security.com
To: user@company.com
Subject: Sign-in attempt blocked — Verify your Microsoft account
Microsoft Account Team, We blocked an unusual sign-in to your Microsoft account from Russia. If this wasn't you, verify your account immediately to prevent unauthorized access: 🔐 Verify Account: http://microsoft-secure-login.xyz/verify If you don't verify within 12 hours, your account access will be revoked. Microsoft Security

🚩 Red Flags Identified

✕Typosquatted domain: "micros0ft" uses zero instead of "o" to evade filters
✕Suspicious link TLD: ".xyz" domain — Microsoft only uses microsoft.com
✕Fear trigger: Claims login from Russia to induce panic and bypass critical thinking
✕12-hour deadline: Artificial urgency designed to prevent verification
⚡Credential harvesting: Clicking "verify" leads to fake login page that steals your password

Example 4 — Legitimate GitHub Notification

SAFE
From: noreply@github.com
To: user@email.com
Subject: [GitHub] Your April 2026 activity summary
Hi username, Here's your GitHub activity summary for April 2026: • 23 commits across 4 repos • 3 pull requests merged • 2 issues closed View your full profile: https://github.com/dashboard Thanks for contributing to open source! The GitHub Team

✅ Why This is Safe

✓Legitimate sender: @github.com is GitHub's real domain
✓Real link: URL goes to github.com — matches sender domain
✓Personal greeting: Uses real username, not generic "Dear User"
✓No urgency: Informational email — no threats, deadlines, or demands

Security Awareness Guide

Everything employees need to know to identify, avoid, and report phishing attacks — in plain, non-technical language.

🎯 What is Phishing?

  • Attackers send fake emails pretending to be trusted organizations (banks, Microsoft, PayPal, HR)
  • Goal: trick you into clicking malicious links, entering passwords, or downloading malware
  • 90% of data breaches start with a phishing email
  • Attacks cost businesses an average of $4.9 million per breach

🔍 How Attackers Think

  • They study your company's tools (Microsoft 365, PayPal, HR portals) and copy their branding
  • They create fake domains that look almost identical (paypa1.com vs paypal.com)
  • They use fear, urgency, and authority to bypass your critical thinking
  • They target employees, not just systems — humans are the weakest link

🚩 Phishing Indicators

  • Sender domain doesn't match the organization (e.g. @paypa1-verify.net vs @paypal.com)
  • Urgent subject lines with threats ("Account will be closed in 24 hours")
  • Generic greetings like "Dear User" or "Valued Customer"
  • Links that don't match the displayed text — always hover before clicking
  • Requests for passwords, OTPs, credit card numbers via email
  • Unexpected attachments (especially .exe, .zip, .docm files)

⚔️ Common Attack Techniques

  • Typosquatting: Fake domains with tiny typos (arnazon.com, micosoft.com)
  • Brand impersonation: Copying logos, colors, and formatting of real companies
  • Urgency manipulation: Artificial deadlines to prevent you from thinking clearly
  • Spear phishing: Personalized attacks using your real name and company info
  • CEO fraud: Pretending to be your boss to request wire transfers

✅ DO'S — Safe Email Practices

  • Verify the sender's full email address, not just the display name
  • Hover over links before clicking to see the real destination URL
  • Go directly to websites by typing the URL — don't click email links
  • Enable Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) on all accounts
  • Report suspicious emails to your IT/security team immediately
  • When in doubt, call the sender on a known number to verify
  • Keep your software and antivirus updated at all times
  • Use a password manager — never reuse passwords across sites

❌ DON'TS — What to Avoid

  • Never click links or download attachments from unknown senders
  • Never enter passwords on pages reached via email links
  • Never share OTPs, passwords, or PINs — legitimate services never ask for these via email
  • Don't trust an email just because it looks professional or has official logos
  • Don't reply to suspicious emails — even replies confirm your address is active
  • Don't use company email for personal accounts (data breaches expose you)
  • Never bypass email security warnings from your mail client
  • Don't assume an urgent email is real — urgency is an attacker's tool
What To Do If You Clicked a Phishing Link
1Disconnect immediately — unplug from WiFi or disable network to stop data exfiltration
2Change your passwords — especially email, banking, and work accounts — from a clean device
3Report to IT Security — notify your security team with a screenshot and the sender address immediately
4Enable MFA on all affected accounts if not already active — this can stop attackers even with your password
5Run a malware scan using your company's approved antivirus tool on the affected device
6Monitor your accounts for unusual activity over the next 30 days — watch for unknown logins or transactions
